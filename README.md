This repository collects the computational material developed for a thesis on **mathematical methods for option pricing**.  The project starts from classical no-arbitrage valuation and then moves progressively towards numerical pricing, stochastic volatility, jump-diffusion dynamics, calibration, and transport-based interpretations of model uncertainty.

The code is intentionally research-oriented.  It is not designed as a production trading library; rather, it is a compact laboratory for comparing different pricing paradigms under a common mathematical language.

---

## Mathematical motivation

Let \((\Omega,\mathcal F,(\mathcal F_t)_{t\ge 0},\mathbb Q)\) be a filtered probability space under a risk-neutral measure \(\mathbb Q\).  For a European call option with maturity \(T\) and strike \(K\), the fundamental valuation identity is

$$
C_0(K,T)=e^{-rT}\,\mathbb E^{\mathbb Q}\!\left[(S_T-K)^+\mid \mathcal F_0\right].
$$

The repository studies different ways of specifying, approximating, and calibrating the conditional distribution of \(S_T\).  In the simplest case, the law of \(S_T\) is induced by geometric Brownian motion.  In more flexible specifications, the terminal law is generated by stochastic volatility, jumps, or feedback effects between asset returns and variance dynamics.

The guiding principle is that option pricing is not merely the computation of a closed-form formula, but the construction of an admissible risk-neutral dynamics consistent with market prices, no-arbitrage restrictions, and numerically stable calibration.

## Implemented pricing blocks

### 1. Black--Scholes benchmark

The Black--Scholes model assumes

$$
\frac{dS_t}{S_t}=(r-q)\,dt+\sigma\,dW_t^{\mathbb Q},
$$

where \(r\) is the continuously compounded risk-free rate, \(q\) is the dividend yield, and \(\sigma\) is constant volatility.  The code implements the standard call/put pricing formula and associated Greeks such as vega, which are also used in calibration weighting.

For a European call,

$$
C^{BS}(S_0,K,T,r,q,\sigma)
= S_0 e^{-qT}\Phi(d_1)-K e^{-rT}\Phi(d_2),
$$

with

$$
d_1=\frac{\log(S_0/K)+(r-q+\frac12\sigma^2)T}{\sigma\sqrt T},
\qquad
 d_2=d_1-\sigma\sqrt T.
$$

### 2. Cox--Ross--Rubinstein binomial tree

The CRR model approximates the continuous-time diffusion by a recombining discrete-time tree.  For \(N\) time steps, \(\Delta t=T/N\),

$$
u=e^{\sigma\sqrt{\Delta t}},
\qquad
d=\nu^{-1},
\qquad
p^*=\frac{e^{(r-q)\Delta t}-d}{\nu-d}.
$$

Backward induction gives

$$
V_{n,j}=e^{-r\Delta t}\left[p^*V_{n+1,j+1}+(1-p^*)V_{n+1,j}\right],
$$

with terminal condition \(V_{N,j}=(S_{N,j}-K)^+\) for calls and \((K-S_{N,j})^+\) for puts.

### 3. Monte Carlo with variance reduction

The Monte Carlo block estimates discounted risk-neutral payoffs by simulation:

$$
\widehat C_M=e^{-rT}\frac{1}{M}\sum_{m=1}^M (S_T^{(m)}-K)^+.
$$

The implementation includes antithetic variates and a control-variate correction.  Given a simulated payoff \(X\), a control variable \(Y\), and known expectation \(\mathbb E[Y]\), the adjusted estimator is

$$
X^{cv}=X+c\left(Y-\mathbb E[Y]\right),
\qquad
c^*=-\frac{\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)}{\operatorname{Var}(Y)}.
$$

This reduces simulation noise and makes the comparison with semi-analytical methods more stable.

### 4. Heston and Heston--Bates dynamics

The stochastic-volatility block is based on a Heston-type variance process, optionally augmented by jumps in the asset price.  A representative risk-neutral specification is

$$
\frac{dS_t}{S_{t^-}}
=\left(r-q-\lambda_J\kappa_J\right)dt+\sqrt{v_t}\,dW_t^{S}+\left(e^{Y}-1\right)dN_t,
$$

$$
dv_t=\kappa(\theta-v_t)dt+\xi\sqrt{v_t}\,dW_t^{v},
\qquad
d\langle W^{S},W^{v}\rangle_t=\rho\,dt,
$$

where \(\kappa\) controls mean reversion, \(\theta\) is long-run variance, \(v_0\) is initial variance, \(\xi\) is volatility of variance, \(\rho\) is the leverage parameter, and \(Y\sim\mathcal N(\mu_J,\sigma_J^2)\) describes lognormal jumps.

The Heston--Bates price is computed through the characteristic function of \(\log S_T\).  The call price can be represented as

$$
C_0=S_0e^{-qT}P_1-Ke^{-rT}P_2,
$$

where \(P_1\) and \(P_2\) are obtained from Fourier inversion of the model characteristic function.

### 5. Stochastic Feedback Volatility \(\mathrm{SFV}\)

The repository also contains an experimental stochastic-volatility-with-feedback specification.  The idea is to extend Heston-type variance dynamics by allowing continuous asset-price innovations to feed directly into variance updates.  In continuous-time notation, a stylised version is

$$
\frac{dS_t}{S_{t^-}}
=\left(r-q-\lambda_S\zeta_S\right)dt
+\sqrt{v_t}\,dW_t^{S}
+\left(e^{J_S}-1\right)dN_t^{S},
$$

$$
dv_t
=\kappa(\theta-v_t)dt
+\xi\sqrt{v_t}\,dW_t^{v}
+\gamma\sqrt{v_t}\,dW_t^{S}
+v_{t^-}\left(e^{J_V}-1\right)dN_t^{V},
$$

with

$$
d\langle W^{S},W^{v}\rangle_t=\rho\,dt.
$$

The parameter \(\gamma\) governs instantaneous feedback from continuous price innovations to variance.  When \(\gamma=0\), the model is closer to a standard stochastic-volatility/jump-diffusion structure; when \(\gamma\ne0\), the variance process reacts more directly to return shocks.

A natural positivity restriction is the effective Feller-type condition

$$
2\kappa\theta \geq \xi^2+\gamma^2+2\rho\xi\gamma.
$$

The code enforces this condition either through projection or by adding a penalty term during calibration.

## Calibration objective

For a set of observed option prices \(\{C_i^{mkt}\}_{i=1}^n\), indexed by strike and maturity, model calibration is formulated as a constrained nonlinear optimisation problem:

$$
\widehat\vartheta
\in
\arg\min_{\vartheta\in\Theta}
\left\{
\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n
w_i\left(C_i^{model}(\vartheta)-C_i^{mkt}\right)^2
+\mathcal R(\vartheta)
\right\}.
$$

Here \(\vartheta\) is the vector of model parameters, \(w_i\) are optional liquidity-, vega-, or moneyness-based weights, and \(\mathcal R\) collects regularisation terms.  The implemented objective functions include, depending on the chosen configuration,

$$
\mathcal R(\vartheta)
=
\lambda_F\left[\max\left\{0,\sigma^2_{eff}-2\kappa\theta\right\}\right]^2
+
\lambda_M
\left(
\frac{e^{-rT}\mathbb E^{\vartheta}[S_T]-S_0e^{-qT}}{S_0e^{-qT}}
\right)^2
+\cdots,
$$

where

$$
\sigma^2_{eff}=\xi^2+\gamma^2+2\rho\xi\gamma.
$$

The first term penalises violations of the effective Feller condition; the second term penalises deviations from the martingale restriction under the risk-neutral measure.

## Transport interpretation

The broader mathematical interpretation of the project is connected to Martingale Optimal Transport and entropy-regularised SchrÃ¶dinger bridge methods.

Given marginal distributions \(\mu_0\) and \(\mu_T\), the martingale transport set is

$$
\Pi_M(\mu_0,\mu_T)
=
\left\{\pi\in\Pi(\mu_0,\mu_T):\mathbb E_\pi[S_T\mid S_0]=S_0\right\}.
$$

A model-free upper price bound for a payoff \(\Phi(S_T)\) can be written as

$$
\overline P(\Phi)
=
\sup_{\pi\in\Pi_M(\mu_0,\mu_T)}
\int \Phi(y)\,d\pi(x,y),
$$

with an analogous lower bound obtained by replacing the supremum with an infimum.  Entropy-regularised variants replace the hard transport problem with a smoother variational problem of the form

$$
\pi^\varepsilon
\in
\arg\min_{\pi\in\Pi_M(\mu_0,\mu_T)}
\left\{
\int c(x,y)\,d\pi(x,y)
+\varepsilon\,\mathrm{KL}(\pi\Vert R)
\right\},
$$

where \(R\) is a reference path measure and \(\varepsilon>0\) controls the strength of entropic regularisation.  This perspective motivates the use of martingale penalties, calibration regularisation, and distributional distance criteria in the numerical implementation.

## Current status

This repository should be read as a **research prototype** accompanying an academic thesis.  The current emphasis is on mathematical modelling, numerical experimentation, and calibration logic rather than package-level software engineering.

The main modelling contributions are:

1. a common implementation framework for classical and stochastic-volatility option pricing models;
2. numerical comparison between closed-form, tree-based, Fourier, and Monte Carlo methods;
3. an experimental feedback-volatility extension of Heston-type dynamics;
4. calibration objectives including pricing errors, regularisation, Feller-type restrictions, and martingale-consistency penalties;
5. a conceptual bridge towards Martingale Optimal Transport and SchrÃ¶dinger bridge formulations.
